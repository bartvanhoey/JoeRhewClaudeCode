### Statement on US government directive to suspend Fable 5 / Mythos 5 access — June 12, 2026
Anthropic released Claude **Fable 5** and Claude **Mythos 5** on June 9, 2026 — days later it received a US government export-control directive ordering it to suspend access to both models "by any foreign national, whether inside or outside the United States." Anthropic disabled access for all customers globally to comply, even though it said it had only received "verbal notice" of a "potential narrow, non-universal jailbreak" (reportedly first flagged by Amazon CEO Andy Jassy) and disagreed this warranted a full recall. This was reportedly the first time the US government has used export-control authority against an AI company's models. A coalition of cybersecurity researchers and executives signed a letter urging the administration to lift the controls.
- Mythos 5 was the more advanced, restricted-access version aimed at cybersecurity professionals/critical-infrastructure operators; Fable 5 was the broader public release with safety guardrails.

## April 2026

### Claude Code performance-decline controversy and public admission — admitted April 24, 2026
After roughly a month of user backlash over a perceived "dumbing down" of Claude Code (some third-party benchmarks, e.g. BridgeMind, showed an Opus-variant accuracy drop from 83.3% to 68.3%), Anthropic published an engineering postmortem on **April 24, 2026** (timed alongside OpenAI's GPT-5.5 launch) acknowledging **three separate engineering missteps**:
1. A lowered default reasoning effort introduced **March 4, 2026**.
2. A caching optimization deployed **March 26, 2026** that inadvertently deleted reasoning history each turn.
3. Tighter text-length/verbosity restrictions that curtailed sustained reasoning.

All three were fixed by **v2.1.116** (as of ~April 20, 2026). Notably, Anthropic had initially implied in its communications that nothing was wrong and that user complaints were overstated — the eventual admission fed broader speculation (including from competitors) that Anthropic was compute-constrained, especially given concurrent outages, peak-hour usage caps, and a limited rollout of the (then-unreleased) Mythos model.
- Sources: [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/04/24/anthropic-engineering-missteps-claude-code-performance-decline-user-backlash/), [Fortune (earlier, backlash coverage)](https://fortune.com/2026/04/14/anthropic-claude-performance-decline-user-complaints-backlash-lack-of-transparency-accusations-compute-crunch/), [BigGo Finance](https://finance.biggo.com/news/202604260452_Anthropic_Admits_Claude_Code_Three_Bugs), [Let's Data Science](https://letsdatascience.com/news/anthropic-confirms-and-fixes-claude-performance-issues-57e98a0b)

## March 2026

### Anthropic sues Trump administration over Pentagon "supply chain risk" designation — filed ~March 9, 2026
Background: On **February 27, 2026**, President Trump directed federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's AI, and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a **"supply chain risk"** — a label typically reserved for foreign adversaries, requiring any DoD-linked contractor to certify it doesn't use Anthropic's models. The dispute stemmed from Anthropic's refusal to drop contractual "red lines" barring use of Claude for autonomous weapons or mass surveillance of US citizens. Anthropic filed **two federal lawsuits**, alleging First and Fifth Amendment violations, APA violations, and that the designation exceeded statutory authority.
- **March 26, 2026:** A federal judge in California **indefinitely blocked** the Pentagon's supply-chain-risk action, ruling it violated Anthropic's constitutional rights (this is the injunction later partially undercut by the April 8 appeals court ruling above — the dispute continued into Q2 2026).

### Bartz v. Anthropic copyright settlement — claims deadline March 30, 2026
The **$1.5 billion** Bartz v. Anthropic settlement (described as the largest copyright settlement in US history) reached its claims-filing deadline on **March 30, 2026**. Background: authors Andrea Bartz, Charles Graeber, and Kirk Wallace Johnson sued Anthropic alleging it downloaded millions of pirated books from shadow libraries (LibGen, PiLiMi) to train its models. In June 2025, Judge William Alsup ruled that training on legally acquired books was fair use, but that training on pirated copies was not — exposing Anthropic to massive statutory damages. Anthropic settled for $1.5B (reportedly ~$3,000/book for ~500,000 works) ahead of a trial that had been slated for December 2025.
- A **separate, new ~$3 billion lawsuit** from major music publishers (Universal, Concord, BMG) alleges "mass torrenting" of songbooks containing lyrics/sheet music tied to at least 714 songs, drawing on evidence unearthed during Bartz discovery. Status as of this research: ongoing, not resolved.
- Sources: [Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/advocacy/artificial-intelligence/what-authors-need-to-know-about-the-anthropic-settlement/), [Lieff Cabraser](https://www.lieffcabraser.com/anthropic-authors-rights/), [Kluwer Copyright Blog](https://legalblogs.wolterskluwer.com/copyright-blog/the-bartz-v-anthropic-settlement-understanding-americas-largest-copyright-settlement/), [Writer Beware (April update)](https://writerbeware.blog/2026/04/10/anthropic-copyright-settlement-april-update/), [CA Courts Newsroom on music-publisher suit](https://newsroom.courts.ca.gov/news/music-publishers-use-evidence-unearthed-bartz-hit-anthropic-3b-suit)
